üìö Teacher's Guide: Lesson 3.4

Why Writing Still Matters
Chapter 3: Prompt Engineering | Discussion Day | Duration: 50 minutes

üéØ Lesson Overview

üîë The Big Idea

This Discussion Day addresses a question students are genuinely asking: "If AI can write, why learn writing?" Through concrete scenarios and structured activities, students discover that writing skills = editorial judgment. You don't need to be a musician to enjoy music, but you DO need musical understanding to improve a song. The same applies to writing and AI.

The breakthrough insight: Editorial skills and writing skills reinforce each other. When you develop editorial judgment by directing AI, you can apply that same judgment to your own writing. This creates a powerful feedback loop where both skillsets grow together.

Why This Lesson Matters

Following Lesson 3.3's insight that personal expertise enables editorial judgment, students need to understand WHY that expertise matters. Many students harbor anxiety that AI makes writing obsolete. This lesson reframes writing skills as MORE valuable, not less‚Äîbut valuable in a different way.

By the end of this lesson, students should understand:

  • Appreciation (Level 1) vs. Editorial Direction (Level 2)
  • Writing skills enable you to diagnose problems and give specific direction
  • Editorial judgment works on AI output AND your own writing
  • Future careers require both writing AND AI whispering skills

‚úÖ What Students Already Know:

  • Four prompt structures and iteration cycles (Lesson 3.2)
  • Strategic context decisions (Lesson 3.3)
  • Personal expertise helps them recognize generic vs. specific output (Lesson 3.3)

üîÆ What This Lesson Prepares Students For:

  • Lesson 3.5: Recognizing Good Output‚Äîapplying editorial judgment in practice
  • Lesson 3.6: Creative project work where they direct AI using editorial skills
  • All future work: Understanding that writing and AI skills grow together, not compete

üéì Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Distinguish between appreciation (Level 1) and editorial direction (Level 2)
  • Explain why understanding a craft enables you to direct improvement, even if someone else does the actual work
  • Articulate how writing skills translate to editorial judgment when working with AI
  • Recognize that editorial skills can improve your own writing through self-application
  • Understand why writing skills are more valuable, not less, in an AI-integrated world
  • Apply these insights to their creative writing project and future career thinking

Mastery Point Alignment

This lesson primarily addresses Mastery Point 2: Prompt Engineering Fundamentals and introduces concepts that will be assessed in Mastery Point 3: Strategic AI Collaboration

  • Level 2: Understands that writing skills help evaluate AI output quality
  • Level 3: Can articulate how understanding craft enables editorial direction
  • Level 4: Recognizes the feedback loop where editorial and writing skills reinforce each other

Materials Needed

  • For Snowball: Prepare 10 teacher-written questions on paper, crumpled (see detailed list below)
  • For Muddiest Points: Blank paper for students, collection method

Before Class: Prepare Your 10 Snowball Questions

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Preparation: The Stealth Snowballs

Write these 10 questions on the same paper students will use. Crumple them. Keep them in your pocket or desk. During the snowball activity, casually toss them into the chaos‚Äîstudents won't notice you're contributing questions. This ensures key concepts get addressed even if student questions miss them.

1. Can someone who never learned to write recognize good writing? Can they make it BETTER? What's the difference?
2. Why do film directors need to understand cinematography even though they don't operate the camera themselves?
3. If you can't write well yourself, can you effectively tell AI what to write for you? Why or why not?
4. What's harder: writing a paragraph yourself, or evaluating whether someone else's paragraph is good and knowing specifically how to improve it?
5. A manager reviews two reports‚Äîone AI-generated, one human-written. How does the manager know which is better if they don't understand good writing?
6. Does learning to cook make you better at choosing restaurants? How is learning to write similar?
7. Why does editing require different skills than writing from scratch? Can someone be a good editor without being a good writer?
8. If AI can write, what human skill becomes MORE valuable, not less?
9. Can you direct someone to do something you don't understand yourself? Give an example.
10. If you develop editorial skills by directing AI, can you use those same skills to improve YOUR OWN writing? How would that work?

üí° Why Question #10 is Critical

This question introduces the feedback loop that's the capstone of the lesson: Editorial judgment developed through AI work transfers back to self-editing. Make sure this question gets discussed, either during Snowball or in your Teacher Synthesis.

üìù Activity Details & Facilitation

Activity 1: Think-Pair-Share (25 minutes)

Setup (2 minutes)

SUGGESTED SCRIPT

"Today we're exploring a question many of you are probably asking: If AI can write well, what's the point of learning writing skills? We're going to approach this through concrete examples rather than abstract discussion."

"Each team is getting one scenario. Your job is to think about what this scenario reveals about the difference between enjoying something and being able to improve it."

[Assign scenarios‚Äîyou can do this via the platform or by calling out team assignments]

Individual Think Time (3 minutes)

What students should be doing:

  • Reading their scenario individually
  • Considering: Can someone enjoy this without being able to improve it?
  • Thinking about what expertise would add

üí° Circulate During Think Time

Use this time to ensure all teams understand their scenario. If a team looks confused, check in: "What's your scenario asking you to think about?" This prevents wasted discussion time.

Team Discussion (7 minutes)

Prompting questions you can offer if teams need guidance:

  • "What's the difference between saying 'I like this' and explaining exactly how to make it better?"
  • "In your scenario, what would expertise allow someone to do that appreciation alone doesn't?"
  • "How would you summarize the key insight from your scenario in one sentence?"

Whole Class Sharing (10 minutes)

‚ö†Ô∏è Keep This Moving

8 teams √ó 1-2 minutes each = 8-16 minutes. Stay on the shorter end (1 minute per team) to prevent dragging. Teams should share:

  • Brief summary of their scenario (15-20 seconds)
  • Key insight they discovered (30-40 seconds)

You can say: "Give us the 30-second version‚Äîwhat's your scenario and what's the key insight?"

Pattern Recognition (3 minutes)

SUGGESTED TRANSITION

"You've just heard 8 different scenarios‚Äîmusic, film, cooking, sports, design, dance, speaking, apps. What pattern did you notice? What did they ALL have in common?"

[Students respond]

"Exactly. In every case, there's a difference between Level 1‚Äîappreciation, saying 'I like this' or 'something's off'‚Äîand Level 2‚Äîeditorial direction, being able to diagnose exactly what's wrong and how to fix it."

"Understanding the craft doesn't just help you DO the craft. It helps you EVALUATE and IMPROVE the craft, whether you're doing it yourself or directing someone else."

Connecting to Writing (5 minutes)

SUGGESTED SCRIPT

"Now let's apply this to writing and AI. Think about your work with Alia on your creative project."

"Level 1 reactions might be: 'This paragraph sounds boring.' 'This is better than the last version.' 'I like this one more.'"

"Level 2 editorial direction sounds like: 'This paragraph is boring because all the sentences are the same length and there are no concrete examples. Make it more engaging by varying sentence structure‚Äîcombine some short sentences with longer ones‚Äîand add a specific story in the second paragraph about someone who actually experienced this problem.'"

"See the difference? Level 1 helps you choose between options. Level 2 lets you tell AI exactly how to make something better."

"Writing skills enable Level 2. The better you understand what makes writing work‚Äîsentence structure, concrete details, paragraph flow, voice, clarity‚Äîthe better you can give AI specific, targeted direction."

"Writing skills equal editorial judgment. You can't whisper effectively if you can't recognize and diagnose good output."

Activity 2: Snowball (15 minutes)

Setup (2 minutes)

SUGGESTED SCRIPT

"Now we're going to explore the implications of this insight through a Snowball activity. Here's how it works:"

"Everyone take out a piece of paper. Write ONE thought-provoking question about writing, AI, editorial judgment, or future skills. Make it a question that needs explanation, not just yes/no."

"When you're done, crumple your paper into a snowball."

[Give students 2 minutes to write]

üí° During Student Writing Time

While students write their questions, unobtrusively crumple YOUR 10 prepared questions. Keep them ready in your hand or pocket.

The Snowstorm (2 minutes)

SUGGESTED SCRIPT

"Alright, everyone stand up with your snowball. On my mark, throw your snowball randomly into the room. Ready? THROW!"

[Chaos! Students throw, you casually toss your 10 into the mix]

"Now everyone pick up ONE snowball‚Äînot your own if you can help it‚Äîand return to your seat."

‚ö†Ô∏è The Stealth Move

During the chaos of throwing, casually toss your 10 teacher-written snowballs into different areas of the room. Students won't notice you're throwing too‚Äîthey're focused on their own snowballs and the general mayhem. This is the beauty of the hybrid approach!

Team Discussion (7 minutes)

Instructions to give students:

  • "Open your snowball and read the question"
  • "Discuss with your team: How would you answer this question?"
  • "Use keywords from today's discussion‚ÄîLevel 1 vs Level 2, appreciation vs direction, editorial judgment"
  • "Be ready to share if called on"

üí° Circulate and Listen

As you move between teams:

  • Listen for which questions are being discussed
  • Note teams with especially rich discussions
  • Identify if any of YOUR questions are being discussed (particularly #10 about the feedback loop)
  • Gently guide teams that are off-track

Whole Class Sharing (4 minutes)

SUGGESTED FACILITATION

"Who caught an interesting question? What does it ask?"

[Student shares question]

"What did your team think? How would you answer it?"

[Student responds, you facilitate brief discussion]

"Anyone else have thoughts on that?"

[Brief exchange, then move to next question]

‚úÖ Strategic Question Selection

If Question #10 (the feedback loop question) hasn't come up naturally, make sure to call on a team that has it: "I want to hear from [Team Name]‚Äîwhat question did you catch?"

This ensures the capstone insight gets aired during the activity, setting up your Teacher Synthesis.

Activity 3: Muddiest Points (5 minutes)

Setup

SUGGESTED SCRIPT

"Before we synthesize everything, I want to make sure we address any confusion. On a piece of paper‚Äîno name required‚Äîwrite down what's still unclear or confusing about the relationship between writing skills and working with AI."

"What questions do you still have? What doesn't quite make sense?"

[Give students 2 minutes to write, then collect papers]

Quick Scan & Address (3 minutes)

What to do:

  • Quickly skim the muddiest points papers
  • Identify the most common confusion themes
  • Address 2-3 of the most common issues briefly
  • If a confusion is complex, acknowledge it: "Great question‚Äîlet's explore that more as we work in the next few labs"

üí° Common Confusions You Might See

  • "But I still don't like writing!" ‚Üí Response: "That's okay. The point isn't that you'll love writing. The point is that understanding writing helps you work better with AI, even if you prefer having AI do the actual writing."
  • "Do I need to be a great writer to use AI well?" ‚Üí Response: "No, but you need enough understanding to recognize quality and give direction. That's different from being a novelist."
  • "Won't AI eventually not need our direction?" ‚Üí Response: "Maybe someday. But even then, you'll need editorial judgment to decide what to use. Someone still has to choose."

üéØ Teacher Synthesis - The Capstone

üîë This Is Where Everything Comes Together

The Teacher Synthesis is critical. Students have explored the concepts through activities, but now you deliver the capstone insight: Editorial skills and writing skills reinforce each other in a feedback loop.

The Synthesis Script (5 minutes)

SUGGESTED SYNTHESIS SCRIPT

[Start with the core recap]

"Let's synthesize what we discovered today. We started with 8 different scenarios‚Äîmusic, film, cooking, sports, design, dance, speaking, apps. Every single one revealed the same pattern: You don't need expertise to enjoy something, but you DO need expertise to improve it."

"We applied that to writing and AI: Writing skills equal editorial judgment. The better you understand what makes writing work, the better you can direct AI to produce what you actually want."

[Now deliver the capstone insight]

"But here's the most powerful part‚Äîand this is what I want you to really understand: This works both ways."

"When you direct AI and develop editorial judgment, those same skills transfer back to your own writing. Think about it: If you can diagnose why AI's paragraph is boring and tell it to vary sentence structure and add concrete details, you can apply that exact same analysis to your own paragraphs."

"Editorial skills and writing skills reinforce each other. It's a feedback loop:"

  • "Understanding writing helps you direct AI better"
  • "Directing AI develops your editorial eye"
  • "Your editorial eye helps you improve your own writing"
  • "Improving your own writing deepens your understanding"
  • "Which helps you direct AI even better"

"They grow together. Every time you give AI specific direction about what to change and why, you're building skills you can use on your own writing. Every time you write something yourself and revise it, you're building skills you can use to direct AI."

[Connect to their experience]

"Think about your creative writing project. When you tell Alia 'This scene needs more tension,' you're practicing the same skill you'd use to revise your own scene. When you write something yourself and realize 'This feels flat‚Äîit needs a concrete sensory detail,' you've just developed editorial judgment you can use with AI."

[Address the career question directly]

"So back to the original question: If AI can write, why learn writing? Because writing skills aren't just about producing text anymore. They're about:

  • "Recognizing quality"
  • "Diagnosing problems"
  • "Giving specific direction"
  • "Making editorial decisions"
  • "Maintaining creative vision"

"Future employers won't ask: 'Can you write?' or 'Can you use AI?' They'll look for people who can do BOTH‚Äîpeople who can direct AI to produce excellent work AND apply editorial judgment to make it even better AND recognize when writing something yourself is more effective than using AI."

[Close with empowerment]

"Writing skills aren't obsolete. They're more valuable than ever‚Äîjust valuable in a different way. You're not competing with AI. You're learning to collaborate with it. And every skill you build in one area strengthens your ability in the other."

"Keep developing both. They're not separate paths‚Äîthey're the same path."

Field Journal Assignment

SUGGESTED SCRIPT

"For your field journal reflection today, think about how your understanding has changed. Before this lesson, you might have thought 'AI can write, so why learn writing?' Now what do you think?"

"Consider: Which scenario from today resonated most with you? How does understanding writing help you direct AI in your creative project? What's one aspect of writing you want to strengthen specifically to improve your editorial judgment?"

"And here's the big one: Can you see how editorial skills you develop by directing AI could improve your own writing? Give a specific example."

If Think-Pair-Share Falls Flat

‚ö†Ô∏è Scenarios Not Landing?

If teams struggle to extract insights from their scenarios:

  • Add a concrete example: "Let's say I love pizza. Can I walk into a pizza kitchen and tell the chef exactly what temperature to bake it at and what ratio of sauce to cheese? No‚ÄîI need cooking knowledge for that."
  • Make it personal: "Has anyone here ever tried to teach someone something you're good at? Sports move? Game strategy? Cooking technique? What made it hard if the other person didn't understand the basics?"
  • Use contrast: "What's the difference between 'This song is good' and 'The chorus is good but the bridge is too long and the key change is jarring‚Äîcut the bridge by half and move the key change earlier'?"

If Snowball Gets Chaotic

üí° Managing the Snowstorm

The throwing part is inherently chaotic‚Äîthat's part of the fun! But if it becomes too unruly:

  • Set boundaries: "Throw across the room, not AT people"
  • Time-box it: "You have 10 seconds to throw and pick up a snowball"
  • Have a signal: "When I say FREEZE, everyone stops and sits down with the snowball they're holding"

If a student didn't get a snowball, have extras (a few of your teacher questions) they can grab.

Ensuring the Feedback Loop Insight Lands

‚úÖ Making the Capstone Stick

The feedback loop (editorial skills ‚Üí writing skills ‚Üí editorial skills) is the most important takeaway. Ensure it lands by:

  • Using gestures: Draw a circle in the air as you describe the feedback loop
  • Drawing on board: Write "Writing Skills ‚Üî Editorial Judgment" with arrows going both ways
  • Using their project: "When you told Alia yesterday to add more sensory details, that's a skill you can now use on your own writing"
  • Asking for examples: "Can someone give me an example of how directing AI taught them something about their own writing?"
