Why Writing Still Matters
Chapter 3: Prompt Engineering | Discussion Day | Duration: 50 minutes (flexible)

üéØ Lesson Overview

üîë The Big Idea

This Discussion Day addresses a question students are genuinely asking: "If AI can write, why learn writing?" Through concrete scenarios and structured activities, students discover that writing skills = editorial judgment. You don't need to be a musician to enjoy music, but you DO need musical understanding to improve a song. The same applies to writing and AI.

The breakthrough insight: Editorial skills and writing skills reinforce each other. When you develop editorial judgment by directing AI, you can apply that same judgment to your own writing. This creates a powerful feedback loop where both skillsets grow together.

Why This Lesson Matters

Following Lesson 3.3's insight that personal expertise enables editorial judgment, students need to understand WHY that expertise matters. Many students harbor anxiety that AI makes writing obsolete. This lesson reframes writing skills as MORE valuable, not less‚Äîbut valuable in a different way.

By the end of this lesson, students should understand:

  • Appreciation (Level 1) vs. Editorial Direction (Level 2)
  • Writing skills enable you to diagnose problems and give specific direction
  • Editorial judgment works on AI output AND your own writing
  • Future careers require both writing AND AI whispering skills

üéì Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Distinguish between appreciation (Level 1) and editorial direction (Level 2)
  • Explain why understanding a craft enables you to direct improvement, even if someone else does the actual work
  • Articulate how writing skills translate to editorial judgment when working with AI
  • Recognize that editorial skills can improve your own writing through self-application
  • Understand why writing skills are more valuable, not less, in an AI-integrated world

üìù Activity Details & Facilitation

Activity 1: Think-Pair-Share - Eight Scenarios (25 minutes)

Purpose

Students discover the pattern that understanding a craft enables improvement across eight different domains (music, film, cooking, sports, design, dance, speaking, apps). This builds the foundation before applying the principle to writing.

Setup (2 minutes)

SUGGESTED SCRIPT

"Today we're exploring a question many of you are probably asking: If AI can write well, what's the point of learning writing skills? We're going to approach this through concrete examples rather than abstract discussion."

"Each team is getting one scenario. Your job is to think about what this scenario reveals about the difference between enjoying something and being able to improve it."

Individual Think Time (3 minutes)

What students should be doing:

  • Reading their scenario individually
  • Considering: Can someone enjoy this without being able to improve it?
  • Thinking about what expertise would add

Team Discussion (7 minutes)

Prompting questions you can offer if teams need guidance:

  • "What's the difference between saying 'I like this' and explaining exactly how to make it better?"
  • "In your scenario, what would expertise allow someone to do that appreciation alone doesn't?"
  • "How would you summarize the key insight from your scenario in one sentence?"

Whole Class Sharing (10 minutes)

‚ö†Ô∏è Keep This Moving

8 teams √ó 1 minute each = 8 minutes. Teams should share:

  • Brief summary of their scenario (15-20 seconds)
  • Key insight they discovered (30-40 seconds)

Pattern Recognition (3 minutes)

SUGGESTED TRANSITION

"You've just heard 8 different scenarios. What pattern did you notice? What did they ALL have in common?"

"Exactly. In every case, there's a difference between Level 1‚Äîappreciation‚Äîand Level 2‚Äîeditorial direction, being able to diagnose exactly what's wrong and how to fix it."

Activity 2: Script Development Transcript (20 minutes)

Purpose

Students see a REAL example of human-AI collaboration where editorial judgment makes the difference. The transcript shows six rounds of refinement, demonstrating that good output requires human direction based on domain knowledge, pedagogical thinking, and editorial expertise.

üìÑ Materials Required

Print and distribute the Script Transcript PDF: Download here

Each team needs one copy of the 3-page transcript.

Setup (2 minutes)

SUGGESTED SCRIPT

"Now we're going to look at a real conversation‚Äînot a made-up example, but an actual conversation between a teacher and AI developing a video script for this course."

"This transcript shows six rounds of collaboration. In each round, the human provides specific editorial direction based on expertise. Your job is to analyze what made that direction possible."

Team Analysis (12 minutes)

Analysis Framework (written on board or displayed):

For each round, identify:

  • Problem/Gap: What specific issue did the human spot?
  • Direction Given: What concrete instruction did they provide?
  • Required Knowledge: What expertise enabled this direction?

üí° Circulate and Guide

Key questions to ask teams:

  • "In Round 2, how did the human catch the math error? What knowledge was required?"
  • "In Round 4, why did adding conceptual bridges matter? Could AI have known to do this?"
  • "In Round 5, the human anticipated student misconceptions. Is that something AI can do on its own?"
  • "Notice Round 6‚Äîthe human makes a judgment call about length vs. value. What makes someone qualified to make that decision?"

Whole Class Discussion (6 minutes)

KEY DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
  • "Which round best shows why editorial expertise matters? Why?"
  • "What kinds of knowledge did the human bring that AI didn't have?"
  • "Could someone without writing/teaching knowledge have given this kind of direction?"
  • "How does this connect to what we learned from the Eight Scenarios?"

‚úÖ Key Takeaway from Activity 2

Students should see that editorial direction requires:

  • Domain expertise (knowing about the subject)
  • Technical knowledge (understanding word counts, timing, structure)
  • Audience awareness (anticipating what students will think)
  • Strategic thinking (balancing competing goals)

All of these come from human understanding‚Äînot just "liking" something.

Activity 3: Snowball (15 minutes)

Purpose

Students generate and explore their own questions about writing, AI, and editorial judgment. This activity surfaces their remaining questions and helps them make personal connections to the concepts.

Setup (2 minutes)

SUGGESTED SCRIPT

"Write ONE thought-provoking question about writing, AI, editorial judgment, or future skills. Make it a question that needs explanation, not just yes/no. When you're done, crumple your paper into a snowball."

The Snowstorm (2 minutes)

SUGGESTED SCRIPT

"Everyone stand up with your snowball. On my mark, throw your snowball randomly into the room. Ready? THROW!"

"Now everyone pick up ONE snowball‚Äînot your own if you can help it‚Äîand return to your seat."

Team Discussion (7 minutes)

Instructions to give students:

  • "Open your snowball and read the question"
  • "Discuss with your team: How would you answer this question?"
  • "Use keywords from today's discussion‚ÄîLevel 1 vs Level 2, appreciation vs direction, editorial judgment"
  • "Be ready to share if called on"

Whole Class Sharing (4 minutes)

FACILITATION APPROACH

Call on 3-4 teams to share interesting questions and their team's thinking. Focus on questions that:

  • Reveal misconceptions worth addressing
  • Connect to the feedback loop concept
  • Show deeper thinking about the concepts

Activity 4: Muddiest Points (5 minutes)

Purpose

Quick formative assessment to identify remaining confusions before the synthesis.

Process

SUGGESTED SCRIPT

"On a piece of paper‚Äîno name required‚Äîwrite down what's still unclear or confusing about the relationship between writing skills and working with AI. What questions do you still have?"

[Give students 2 minutes to write, then collect papers]

[Quickly skim and address 2-3 most common confusions in 3 minutes]

üí° Common Confusions You Might See

  • "But I still don't like writing!" ‚Üí "That's okay. Understanding writing helps you work better with AI, even if you prefer having AI do the actual writing."
  • "Do I need to be a great writer?" ‚Üí "No, but you need enough understanding to recognize quality and give direction."
  • "Won't AI eventually not need our direction?" ‚Üí "Maybe someday. But even then, someone has to choose. That requires judgment."

üéØ Teacher Synthesis - The Capstone

üîë This Is Where Everything Comes Together

The Teacher Synthesis is critical regardless of which activities you chose. This is where you deliver the capstone insight: Editorial skills and writing skills reinforce each other in a feedback loop.

The Synthesis Script (5 minutes)

SUGGESTED SYNTHESIS SCRIPT

[Start with the core recap]

"Today we discovered that understanding a craft enables you to improve it‚Äîwhether through the Eight Scenarios, the Script Transcript, or both. You don't need expertise to enjoy something, but you DO need expertise to make it better."

"We applied that to writing and AI: Writing skills equal editorial judgment. The better you understand what makes writing work, the better you can direct AI."

[Now deliver the capstone insight]

"But here's the most powerful part: This works both ways."

"When you direct AI and develop editorial judgment, those same skills transfer back to your own writing. If you can diagnose why AI's paragraph is boring and tell it to vary sentence structure, you can apply that exact same analysis to your own paragraphs."

"Editorial skills and writing skills reinforce each other. It's a feedback loop:"

  • "Understanding writing ‚Üí helps you direct AI better"
  • "Directing AI ‚Üí develops your editorial eye"
  • "Your editorial eye ‚Üí helps you improve your own writing"
  • "Improving your own writing ‚Üí deepens your understanding"
  • "Which helps you direct AI even better"

[Connect to their experience]

"Think about your creative writing project. When you tell Alia 'This scene needs more tension,' you're practicing the same skill you'd use to revise your own scene."

[Address the career question directly]

"So: If AI can write, why learn writing? Because writing skills aren't just about producing text anymore. They're about recognizing quality, diagnosing problems, giving specific direction, making editorial decisions, and maintaining creative vision."

"Future employers will look for people who can do BOTH‚Äîdirect AI to produce excellent work AND apply editorial judgment to make it even better."

[Close with empowerment]

"Writing skills aren't obsolete. They're more valuable than ever‚Äîjust valuable in a different way. Keep developing both. They're not separate paths‚Äîthey're the same path."

Field Journal Assignment

SUGGESTED SCRIPT

"For your field journal reflection, think about how your understanding changed today. Consider: Which activity or insight resonated most? How does understanding writing help you direct AI? What's one writing skill you want to strengthen specifically to improve your editorial judgment? And can you see how editorial skills you develop by directing AI could improve your own writing?"
